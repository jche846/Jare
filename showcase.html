Addressing Drought in the South Island

Project Showcase

Prepared by Aquagate 2015

The Drought Problem

On average, New Zealand receives plentiful rainfall each year. Despite our wealth of water, a large portion of NZ’s rain falls in the West Coast in contrast to the arid farmland in Otago and Canterbury. Recognising that much of the NZ’s export income derived from the agriculture sector, a severe drought would be detrimental to NZ’s economic stability by impacting GDP by over $5 billion.

Agricultural and dairy farmers are increasingly demanding more water to become readily available due to their economic contribution. With usually a once-a-year income, the loss of a year’s stock or harvest via drought have major economic effects for the farmers and the wider economy.

In response to this problem, Aquagate will conduct a feasibility study on the proposed pipeline under the Southern Alps that would supply water from the water-rich West Coast to the dry farmland of Otago and Canterbury.

Best Fit Solution

The Whitcombe Valley Trans-Alpine Water Scheme provides water from the Rakaia River via a tunnel through Bonds Peak from the catchment situated at end of the Whitcombe River. This project is estimated at $3.3 billion dollars and will be completed in a span of 7 years. This solution addresses the problem of drought in the East side of the South Island affecting the Canterbury and Northern Otago. The catchment captures 7 metres of rain and 3 metres of snow melt, providing enough water to cover peak demands in times of droughts in the farmlands.

A hydropower dam will be created at the end of the Hokitika-Whitcombe River with the spillway gates facing the river. The dam is expected to generate 724GWh of power. The dam will also control potential spillage of water during heavy rainfall periods to ensure that the water does not flood the Hokitika-Whitcombe River. The naming of the dam and the reservoir created in this project will be chosen at the completion of the project by Ngāti Waewae and Ngāi Tahu, the local iwis of the West Coast-Hokitika region.

The 6 metre diameter tunnel is 7.3km long and has a fall of 0.8° from the inlet altitude of 750 meters to the outlet altitude of 700 meters. A 4 metre diameter high-density polyethylene (HDPE) pipe will be placed in the tunnel allowing an average of 50m³/s of water to flow during drought conditions. The tunnel is situated away from the Southern Alps fault line but will be designed to ensure minimal impact to earthquake and shear translation.

This solution meets the majority of the stakeholder’s requirements providing a long term solution for drought-weary farmers and has the least amount of environmental impact. The local iwis and the Department of Conservation will be consulted throughout each stage of the project to ensure that proper consents and regulation processes are implemented and minimal disturbances to the surrounding environment, animal habitat and river systems.
